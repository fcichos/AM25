    Toner-Tu Model for Flocking

    From Vicsek to Toner-Tu

    The Toner-Tu model provides a hydrodynamic description of flocking phenomena (think birds, fish schools, or cell migrations). It was developed to explain how the discrete Vicsek model, where particles align their velocities with neighbors, leads to collective motion at the macroscopic scale.

    The Hydrodynamic Equations

    The Toner-Tu equations for the density field \(\rho(\mathbf{r},t)\) and velocity field \(\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{r},t)\) are:

    \[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\rho \mathbf{v}) = 0\]

    \[\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + \lambda_1 (\mathbf{v} \cdot \nabla)\mathbf{v} = \alpha \mathbf{v} - \beta |\mathbf{v}|^2 \mathbf{v} - \nabla P + D_1 \nabla^2 \mathbf{v} + D_2 \nabla(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{v}) + ...\]

    where: - The first equation represents mass conservation - In the second equation: - \(\alpha \mathbf{v} - \beta |\mathbf{v}|^2 \mathbf{v}\) drives the velocity to a preferred magnitude \(|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\alpha/\beta}\) - \(\nabla P\) is the pressure gradient, often written as \(P = P_0 + P_1 \rho + P_2 \rho^2 + ...\) - \(D_1, D_2\) are diffusion coefficients

    Key Predictions: Breaking Equilibrium Constraints

    The Toner-Tu model makes several remarkable predictions that distinguish flocking from equilibrium systems:

    1. Long-range order in 2D: Unlike equilibrium systems constrained by the Mermin-Wagner theorem, flocks can maintain true long-range order even in two dimensions.

    2. Giant number fluctuations: Density fluctuations are anomalously large, scaling as \(\Delta N \sim N^{a}\) with \(a > 1/2\) (the equilibrium value).

    3. Sound waves: Perturbations propagate as sound modes with anisotropic speed, faster along the direction of flock motion.

    Active Field Theories

    Active Model A/B Classification

    Active field theories extend the classification of equilibrium dynamics (Model A for non-conserved, Model B for conserved fields) to active systems by adding non-equilibrium terms:

    Active Model A (non-conserved order parameter \(\phi\)): \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = -\Gamma \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \phi} + \lambda(\phi, \nabla\phi, ...) + \eta\]

    Active Model B (conserved order parameter \(\phi\)): \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left(\Gamma \nabla \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \phi} + \boldsymbol{\Lambda}(\phi, \nabla\phi, ...) + \boldsymbol{\eta}\right)\]

    where \(\mathcal{F}\) is an effective free energy, and \(\lambda\) or \(\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\) are active terms that cannot be derived from a free energy.

    Motility-Induced Phase Separation (MIPS)

    A canonical example of active phase separation is MIPS, where purely repulsive self-propelled particles spontaneously phase separate due to a density-dependent mobility.

    The continuum description can be formulated as a modified Cahn-Hilliard equation:

    \[\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left(M(\phi) \nabla \frac{\delta \mathcal{F}}{\delta \phi} + \mathbf{v}(\phi) \phi\right)\]

    where \(M(\phi)\) is the mobility, \(\mathcal{F}\) is an effective free energy, and \(\mathbf{v}(\phi)\) represents the active current that depends on density.

    Linear Stability Analysis in Active Systems

    General Methodology

    Linear stability analysis is essential for understanding when and how homogeneous states become unstable in active systems:

    1. Start with a homogeneous steady state solution
    2. Add small perturbations: \(\phi(\mathbf{r},t) = \phi_0 + \delta\phi e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r} + \sigma t}\)
    3. Linearize the equations to find the dispersion relation \(\sigma(\mathbf{k})\)
    4. If Re(\(\sigma\)) > 0 for any wavevector \(\mathbf{k}\), the system is unstable

    Active Instabilities

    Several instabilities are characteristic of active systems:

    1. Bend instability in extensile active nematics (\(\zeta < 0\)): The growth rate of bend deformations scales as: \[\sigma \sim -K_b q^2 + |\zeta| q^2\] where \(K_b\) is the bend elastic constant. When activity exceeds elasticity (\(|\zeta| > K_b\)), the system becomes unstable.

    2. Splay instability in contractile active nematics (\(\zeta > 0\)): Similarly, contractile systems preferentially develop splay deformations.

    3. Active phase separation in scalar active matter: The dispersion relation shows an activity-induced instability band at finite wavelengths.

    Experimental Realizations and Tests

    Continuum theories provide testable predictions for experiments:

    1. Microtubule-kinesin mixtures: Exhibit active turbulence with self-propelling +1/2 defects
    2. Bacterial suspensions: Show active turbulence and collective motion as predicted by hydrodynamic theories
    3. Cell monolayers: Display behavior consistent with active elasticity and active phase separation
    4. Synthetic active colloids: Demonstrate motility-induced phase separation

    Key experimental measurements that test continuum theories include:

    • Velocity correlation functions and their decay with distance
    • Defect statistics and dynamics
    • Structure factors and scaling laws
    • Sound propagation in active systems
